What is Concentrated Stock Risk?

At its core, concentrated stock risk is a form of unsystematic risk. While systematic risk affects the entire market (e.g., interest rate changes, geopolitical events), unsystematic risk is specific to a single company or industry. It's the risk that your company's stock could plummet due to factors that don't impact the broader market, such as a failed product launch, regulatory trouble, or an accounting scandal.

When a large portion of your net worth is tied to one stock, your financial well-being is tethered to the fortunes of that single entity. The same forces that drove the stock's incredible growth can also lead to a swift and devastating decline, erasing years of accumulated wealth. Diversification is the time-tested antidote to this specific risk.

Risk, Diversifiable and Not

To grasp the threat of a concentrated position, it's helpful to separate investment risk into two distinct categories.

  1. Systematic Risk: This is the inherent risk of being in the market itself—the kind that impacts all companies and can't be eliminated through diversification. Think of recessions, shifts in interest rate policy, or broad geopolitical events.
  2. Unsystematic Risk: Also known as company-specific risk, this is the danger that is unique to a single company or its industry. This is the risk you can—and often should—mitigate through diversification.

Sources of Company-Specific Risk

Unsystematic risk can materialize suddenly and without warning from many sources:

  • Competitive Disruption: A rival company launches a superior product or technology.
  • Regulatory Headwinds: New laws are passed that harm the company’s business model.
  • Leadership Crisis: A key executive departs unexpectedly or the leadership team makes a critical strategic error.
  • Operational Failures: Issues like accounting irregularities can shatter investor confidence overnight.
